# AIisms to Avoid

A list of phrases, tics and structural moves that signal generic AI prose. None of these appear in the source. None should appear in the new draft.

## Phrasal AIisms (banned outright)
- "couldn't help but" (smile, notice, feel)
- "in that moment"
- "a wave of [emotion] washed over her"
- "a single tear rolled down her cheek"
- "her heart shattered into a million pieces"
- "tears streamed down her face"
- "she let out a breath she didn't know she'd been holding"
- "the weight of the world on her shoulders"
- "a bittersweet smile"
- "a mix of [X] and [Y]"
- "she couldn't shake the feeling that…"
- "time seemed to stand still"
- "the silence was deafening"
- "her mind raced"
- "a lump formed in her throat"
- "she swallowed hard"
- "her vision blurred with tears"

## Structural AIisms
- **Mood-setting weather.** No rain on the windowpane, grey skies, sun refusing to shine. The source never names the weather.
- **Symbolic objects introduced and then explained.** A daisy is a daisy. Do not write "the daisy, a symbol of her brother's innocence, lay in her hand." Let the object work without an asterisk.
- **Climactic interior monologue.** Cleo does not have a five-sentence realisation at the graveside. She has one line of dialogue.
- **Tidy resolution.** No "and slowly, she began to heal." No "she knew she would carry him with her forever, and that was enough." The TED Talk scene is the resolution, and it is strange, not soothing.
- **Twin-stating.** Do not have the narration repeatedly call them "the twins." The word "twins" appears once, inside a nickname, inside dialogue.
- **The author's wink.** Do not have a character notice the wordplay. "Insufferable, get it? Because we were inseparable." Cut. Always cut.

## Adverb and tag AIisms
- Avoid emotional adverbs in speech tags: "she said tearfully," "he whispered brokenly," "she replied softly," "he murmured tenderly." Use "said" or no tag at all.
- Avoid stacking gerunds for atmosphere: "Sighing, she turned, brushing a tear from her cheek, remembering the way he…" — none of this.
- Avoid the dialogue–action sandwich on every line. Most lines should be naked.

## Em-dash and ellipsis discipline
- The source uses em-dashes and ellipses **very sparingly** and for specific effects (a character revising themselves mid-sentence; a character trailing off mid-thought). Do not pepper the prose with em-dashes for rhythmic decoration. One per scene, maximum, and only where the grammar demands it.
- Do not use ellipses to imply emotion the prose hasn't earned.

## Body-language AIisms
- No "she nodded slowly." No "her eyes filled with tears." No "he gave her a small, sad smile." No "she let her head fall against his shoulder." The source contains almost no physical choreography around dialogue. Resist filling it in.

## Length AIism
- **Do not pad.** If a scene can end on its sharpest line, end it there. The whole piece is around 600 words. Anything over 800 is wrong. If the draft is running long, the cuts are not in the dialogue — they are in the narration around the dialogue.

## The "wise narrator" AIism
- The story has no narrator who knows what it all means. Do not slip in sentences like "Grief, she would learn, was the price of love." No aphorisms. No earned wisdom. The characters do not understand what is happening to them, and the prose does not understand it either. That is the whole point.
