Write a short story between 400 and 500 words using the following information

# Writing Style

## Length and shape
- **Around 600 words total.** Not 800. Not 1,200. The brevity is the form.
- Four scenes, divided by a horizontal rule of dashes (`--------------------------`).
- Scene lengths are uneven on purpose: scene 1 is the longest; scenes 2 and 3 are very short; scene 4 is the shortest of all and ends the story on two words of dialogue.

## Prose
- **Dialogue carries the story.** The majority of every scene is spoken. Narration is the connective tissue, not the body.
- Narration is spare and functional, with occasional single lyrical lines — the flowers list, the dress sitting on Cleo "like a crumpled shadow," the hat "festooned with daisies set jauntily on top." One per scene, no more.
- **Almost no speech tags.** Where they appear, they are plain ("Cleo's voice rose against the silence…"). No "she murmured tearfully," no choreographed beats around every line.
- Sentence rhythm is short. Long sentences are reserved for one or two moments of release (the flowers list; the pinning of the sleeve).
- **Self-correction inside sentences is a deliberate device.** "We said Leo was your right arm, and that is – was – true." Let characters revise themselves mid-line. The grammar carries the grief.

## Imagery and motif (these are load-bearing, not decoration)
- **The empty sleeve / the missing arm.** Physical, recurrent, never melodramatic. Pinned, not hidden, not displayed.
- **Black satin.** The dress in scenes 1 and 3 becomes a suit in scene 4. The fabric persists; the cut changes. Cleo grows up inside the same material.
- **Leo's flower (daisies).** It migrates from his graveside (clutched in her one arm) to her hat (a crown). Grief becomes ornament becomes identity.
- **Mirrors / reflections.** Cleo only appears in a mirror in the final scene. Her ability to look at herself directly marks the arc.
- **The right side / the right arm.** Repeatedly invoked. Leo *was* the right arm. The parents promise to *be* the right arm. By the end Cleo *carries* the right arm inside her, by carrying Leo.

## Wordplay (core, not decoration)
Three puns hold the story up. Do not telegraph any of them; let the reader land on them.

1. **The twins' names.** Rhyme and overlap by a single letter (the source uses **Cleo / Leo**). The near-overlap is the architecture; on the page, separating them feels almost violent.
2. **The father's nickname for the twins.** A near-pun on a word meaning "cannot be separated." The source uses **"the insufferable twins"** for "inseparable." The joke is dark and loving at once — pick a word with the same double-edge.
3. **The TED Talk title.** A near-pun on **"Life after Death,"** substituting a word that means inner depth or descent. The source uses **"Life after Depth."** Drop it in the announcement, no commentary.

Optional fourth: a "veil of tears" / "vale of tears" echo when describing the mother's face in scene 1.

## Time
- **Time jumps are unmarked.** The reader must infer the gap between the funeral and the TED Talk. Do not date-stamp anything. Do not write "Years later," "Two years on," "After a long time," etc.

## Endings
- **Every scene ends on a line of dialogue or a single image**, never on summary.
- The story's last line is two words. Do not append anything after it. No coda. No closing image. No final breath.

# Characters

Four named characters. Two voices do most of the work (the daughter and the mother). The father has only a handful of lines. The dead brother does not speak — except, possibly, at the very end through his sister.

The twins' names should rhyme and differ by a single letter (the source uses **Cleo** / **Leo**). This near-overlap is structural: it should feel almost cruel to pull them apart on the page.

---

## Cleo — the surviving twin

**Body and dress.** Conjoined with her brother at birth; surgically separated as a baby; the separation left her with only her left arm. Normally dresses in bright, clashing colours. The black mourning dress sits wrong on her — "like a crumpled shadow" is the kind of image the prose reaches for. Twenty-something or younger; old enough to give a TED Talk by the end.

**Voice.**
- Raw, blunt, almost childlike at moments. She says the unsayable thing first ("Why can't I be the dead one?") and then audibly corrects herself mid-line ("Yes, I, I… no.").
- Uses the **present tense about her dead brother** and resists past-tense framings. "He was in me. He is in me." She is correcting her mother as much as herself.
- Repetition for force. Doublings, triplings of the same word.
- Asks questions a child would ask but carrying adult weight: "Did Leo really steal my arm?" / "What am I now? What is Leo?"
- At the graveside she produces a **chiastic farewell** — roughly "You are in me. I am in you." This is the only line she gives the world there.
- By the final scene her speech has compressed. She addresses Leo and answers for both of them in one breath. Two short lines, one of which is two words.

**Arc.** Not "recovery." **Incorporation.** She does not stop being a duo; she becomes the duo.

---

## Anita — the mother

**Function.** Holds the household together at the cost of holding herself together. POV anchor for scenes 1–3.

**Voice — with Cleo.**
- Gentle, redirective, practical. Where Cleo lurches toward the abyss, Anita offers a handhold: "Let me be a bit of Leo and help you get dressed."
- Reframes loss without denying it. Will say something like "We said Leo was your right arm, and that is – was – true." Note the visible self-correction inside the sentence; she lets the grammar do the grieving.
- Will use the father's family nickname for the twins (a near-pun on "inseparable") even on the worst day, because lightness is also a form of love.
- Speaks in absolutes when Cleo asks existential questions, and ends those absolutes with repetition: "Always! Always always!"

**Voice — with her husband (scene 2).**
- Register collapses. Unguarded. "My heart is ripped right out, but it's still beating, somewhere. Yet I'm still alive."
- Short fragments, run-ons, contradictions. The opposite of how she speaks to Cleo.

---

## Adrian — the father

**Presence.** Off-page for most of the story. Named only at the graveside.

**Voice.** The likely source of the nickname "the insufferable twins" — a dark, loving pun that has clearly been household currency for years. In the parents' scene he (presumably) makes the surgery comparison: "it's like we are back in the surgery, waiting for their separation. Hoping. Not knowing." Spare, image-driven, no self-pity.

He should not have more than three or four lines in the entire story. He carries weight by not speaking.

---

## Leo — the deceased twin

**Presence.** Never speaks in his own voice. Present as **absence and as anatomy:** he was Cleo's right arm.

**One personal detail.** He loved daisies (use whatever simple, unfashionable flower fits — but pick one and let it become a motif).

**The final line.** In the closing scene, "Leo" speaks one line. The prose deliberately does not confirm whether it is Cleo's own voice answering herself, her interior brother, or the reader's projection. **Preserve the ambiguity. Do not resolve it.**

# Locations

Four locations, all minimally rendered. The story is not interested in rooms; it is interested in people inside them. If a setting detail does not echo a motif (the sleeve, the daisies, the black satin, the mirror) or carry dialogue, leave it out.

---

## 1. Cleo's room — scene 1
A doorway and a frame (the mother clutches one). Implied bed or chair where the dress is being put on. Nothing else is named. The black satin dress and the empty pinned sleeve are the only objects that get any prose.

## 2. An unnamed private space — scene 2
**Not described at all.** Pure dialogue. The reader infers two parents alone together; nothing else. No furniture, no time of day, no room.

## 3. The graveside — scene 3
One sentence of flowers: bouquets of bright spring/funeral blooms laid before the foot of the grave, named in a small list (the source uses daffodils, tulips, roses, carnations — pick four).

Leo's flower (daisies, or whatever simpler bloom you chose) is held separately, in Cleo's one remaining arm.

**No mourners are described. No priest, no eulogy, no readings, no weather.** The cemetery is whatever the reader brings to it.

## 4. Backstage at a TED Talk venue — scene 4
A mirror. An offstage speaker. No description of the venue or the audience. The outfit and the announcement do the work that a set would.

---

## Rule of thumb
Every location is a frame for an object or a voice. Describe the frame in one phrase, then move.

# Scene Beats

A short story in four scenes, separated by a section divider (`--------------------------`).

**Tense:** past throughout.
**Person:** third.
**Distance:** close but disciplined. One interior line per scene at most, sometimes none. Dialogue does the rest.
**POV migration:** anchored to the mother in scenes 1–3, shifts to the daughter in scene 4. The migration mirrors the theme — the mother holds the camera while the daughter is breaking; the daughter takes the camera once she has reconstituted herself.
**Total length:** ~600 words. The brevity is the form.

---

## Scene 1 — The dressing
**POV:** mother, close third. **Length:** the longest scene; most of the story's word count.

Setting: an unspecified private room, day of a funeral.

- Mother appears in a doorway and asks if her daughter needs help.
- Daughter, struggling with a black mourning dress, says she wishes she were the dead one — then audibly walks it back, half-correcting herself mid-sentence.
- Through dialogue and small physical action, the reader gradually learns: the daughter and her dead brother were conjoined twins, surgically separated as babies; the daughter has only her left arm; the brother had been her right arm both literally (before separation) and figuratively (as a sibling).
- Mother crosses to her, frees the daughter's arm from the dress, and pins the empty sleeve so it neither hides nor showcases the missing limb.
- Dialogue surfaces a family nickname for the twins — a near-pun on a word meaning "cannot be separated," coined by the father.
- Scene ends with the daughter asking what she is now, and what her brother is now. The mother answers in absolutes: she is still their daughter; he is still their son and her brother. Always.

**Ends on:** identity, asserted by the mother against the daughter's vertigo.

---

## Scene 2 — The parents
**POV:** ambiguous, near-pure dialogue. **Length:** very short — eight lines or so.

Setting: unspecified private moment. Likely later the same day.

- A stripped-down exchange between two adults — clearly the parents. No attributions, no stage directions, no setting.
- Topic: how the daughter is coping (badly); how the speakers themselves are coping (not).
- Closes with one parent likening the present grief to the original separation surgery — back in the operating theatre, waiting, hoping, not knowing. The other simply says "Yes."

**Ends on:** the present grief recognised as a re-experience of the original wound.

---

## Scene 3 — The graveside
**POV:** mother, lyrical close third. **Length:** one short paragraph plus one line of dialogue.

Setting: a cemetery. The funeral itself is not staged — no priest, no mourners, no readings, no weather.

- A single sentence of imagery: bouquets of bright spring/funeral flowers (specific named varieties) laid before the grave.
- The mother's gaze fixes on her daughter, who clutches her brother's favourite flower (a different, simpler bloom) in her one remaining arm, pressed against the same black satin dress from scene 1.
- One internal line from the mother: she suppresses the urge to throw herself into the grave because her daughter and husband need her. The husband is named here for the first time.
- The daughter speaks aloud over the silence — a single chiastic farewell that asserts the brother lives inside her and she inside him.

**Ends on:** her voice rising against silence; mutual interiorisation.

---

## Scene 4 — The TED Talk
**POV:** daughter, close third. **Length:** the shortest of all — a beat, not a scene.

Setting: backstage at a public talk. Time has clearly passed. The interval is unmarked: could be a year, could be a decade.

- The daughter checks her reflection in a mirror: a black satin suit, black stilettoes, a black lacy hat festooned with the brother's flower, set jauntily on top. The outfit echoes and elevates the funeral dress; the flower echoes the graveside.
- An offstage speaker announces the next TED Talk, billed as a joint act by both siblings, on a topic whose title is a one-letter pun on "Life after Death."
- The daughter asks, into the air: "Are we ready, [brother's name]?"
- A reply comes — two words — and the prose deliberately does not confirm whether the daughter spoke both lines herself, whether she has internalised her brother as a continuing voice, or both. The story ends on the reply.

**Ends on:** the duo restored, on her terms. Two words. Do not add anything after.

# Do This, But Don't Do This

## Structure
- **Do** open in the middle of an action — the mother appearing in the doorway — not with backstory.
- **Don't** open with a flashback to the conjoined-twin surgery, a hospital scene, a birth scene, or a "we were always different from other siblings" preamble.
- **Do** let the reader piece together what happened to Cleo's arm across the first scene, through dialogue and gesture.
- **Don't** explain the conjoined-twin history in narration. No "Cleo had been born conjoined to her twin brother Leo, and at six months old the surgeons…" Let the reader work.
- **Do** keep the parents' scene short and stripped — pure dialogue, no setting, no attribution, eight lines or so.
- **Don't** stage the funeral. No coffin description, no eulogy, no priest, no crowd, no weather, no procession. The graveside scene is one paragraph plus one line of dialogue.
- **Do** end with a two-word line of dialogue. The penultimate line is a question to Leo; the final line is the answer.
- **Don't** add a closing paragraph, an epilogue, a coda, or a thematic summing-up. The two-word line is the ending.

## Voice and dialogue
- **Do** allow Cleo to be raw, ungrammatical, contradictory ("Yes, I, I… no.") and to use present tense about her dead brother.
- **Don't** have Cleo deliver a polished or articulate grief monologue. She is splintering, not narrating.
- **Do** let Anita reframe gently and physically — pinning the sleeve, offering to "be a bit of Leo" while she helps with the dress.
- **Don't** have Anita cry openly to Cleo or collapse in front of her. She saves her unguarded grief for the scene with her husband.
- **Do** let characters say the same word twice when it is the right word ("Always! Always always!"). Repetition reads as feeling, not as laziness.
- **Don't** have characters explain the puns or nicknames. Anita does not say "your father calls you that because you were inseparable." She just says the nickname; the reader hears the second meaning.

## Disability
- **Do** treat the missing arm matter-of-factly. The empty sleeve is pinned, not hidden, not displayed. Cleo carries flowers in her left arm. She gets dressed by herself with help.
- **Don't** lean on the missing arm as tragedy. Don't have characters stare at it, comment on it from outside, or describe it through a stranger's eyes. Don't use words like "deformity," "stump," "disabled." The story never does.

## Grief
- **Do** physicalise grief, but in one image per character, sparingly. Anita: her heart "ripped right out, but it's still beating, somewhere."
- **Don't** use generic grief language: "overwhelming sadness," "her world came crashing down," "she had never felt such pain," "the weight of his absence." None of that.

## The ending twist
- **Do** make it ambiguous whether Cleo speaks both lines herself, or whether Leo has somehow continued inside her. Leave it open.
- **Don't** confirm it. No "she heard his voice in her head." No "and Leo, somehow, answered." No "she smiled, knowing he was with her." The reader supplies the explanation.

## Titles
- **Do** give the piece a one-word title with a double meaning that points to twoness (the source is "Duo").
- **Don't** title it "Two Halves," "One Arm, Two Hearts," "Forever Twins," "The Other Half," "Sister," etc. Theme-announcing titles kill the story.
